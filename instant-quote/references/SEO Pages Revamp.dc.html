Designing FDM parts that don’t fail: walls, orientation, tolerances

Most FDM parts that fail in service fail for one of three design reasons: walls that ignore how the printer deposits material, loads that pull layer bonds apart instead of running along them, or fits specified as if the part were machined. None of these are printing defects. All of them are decided in CAD, before the file ever reaches a printer.

This guide is the checklist we apply when a model lands in our queue. Everything below assumes a 0.4 mm nozzle and the materials from our catalog — PETG, ASA, and PA12-CF.

01Walls: design in whole beads

An FDM printer builds walls from extruded beads roughly 0.4–0.45 mm wide. The slicer fills your wall with as many whole beads as fit. A 1.0 mm wall doesn’t get 2.5 beads — it gets two beads and a gap-fill pass, which is slower to print and weaker than the number in your CAD suggests.

So design wall thickness in multiples of the bead width: 0.8, 1.2, 1.6, 2.0 mm. The practical minimums we hold to:

MaterialMinimum wallStructural recommendation
PETG1.2 mm1.6–2.0 mm
ASA1.2 mm1.6–2.0 mm
PA12-CF1.0 mm1.2–1.6 mm

PA12-CF gets away with less because the fiber-filled bead is much stiffer; PETG and ASA below 1.2 mm flex visibly and transmit light. Below these minimums a wall isn’t just weak — it’s unpredictable, because a single missed bead is a large fraction of the cross-section.

02Strength is directional

A printed part is a laminate. Within a layer, loads run along continuous beads of solid polymer. Between layers, loads cross a thermal weld that formed in a fraction of a second. That weld is the weak link: tensile strength across layers (Z) lands at roughly 50–80% of the in-plane (XY) figure — and fatigue performance drops further.

The safe design assumption: take the datasheet tensile number and halve it for any load that crosses layers. PETG is the most forgiving of our materials here; ASA and PA12-CF give up more in Z.

What tolerances can you actually expect from FDM?

As-printed tolerance classes, what drives the variance, and a reference clearance table for press, slide and loose fits.

Ask three print shops what tolerance FDM holds and you’ll get three different answers, because the honest answer is conditional: it depends on the material, the feature, and the direction. This article gives the numbers we actually commit to on production parts — and a clearance table you can design against today.

The short version
Treat FDM as a ±0.3 mm per 100 mm process, plan fits with deliberate clearance, and machine the one feature that genuinely needs better.

01As-printed tolerance classes

These are the bands we hold across real geometries — enclosures, brackets, housings — not best-case test cubes:

FeaturePETG / ASAPA12-CF
General dimensions (per 100 mm)±0.3 mm±0.25 mm
Small features (< 20 mm)±0.15 mm±0.12 mm
Vertical hole diameter−0.2 to −0.4 mm−0.2 to −0.3 mm
Flatness (per 100 mm)0.3–0.5 mm0.2–0.4 mm
Z height (per 100 mm)±0.2 mm±0.2 mm

Read the hole row carefully: printed holes are systematically undersize, not randomly off. Systematic errors are the good kind — you compensate for them in CAD, as covered in our FDM design guide.

Designing FDM parts that don’t fail: walls, orientation, tolerances

MICRO_FACTORY 2026-07-16 8 min read
Contents · 7 sections

Most FDM parts that fail in service fail for one of three design reasons: walls that ignore how the printer deposits material, loads that pull layer bonds apart instead of running along them, or fits specified as if the part were machined.

01Walls: design in whole beads

So design wall thickness in multiples of the bead width: 0.8, 1.2, 1.6, 2.0 mm. The practical minimums we hold to:

MaterialMinimum wallStructural rec.
PETG1.2 mm1.6–2.0 mm
ASA1.2 mm1.6–2.0 mm
PA12-CF1.0 mm1.2–1.6 mm
