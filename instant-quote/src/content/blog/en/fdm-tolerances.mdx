Ask three print shops what tolerance FDM holds and you'll get three different answers, because the honest answer is conditional: it depends on the material, the feature, and the direction. This article gives the numbers we actually commit to on production parts ‚Äî and a clearance table you can design against today.

## As-printed tolerance classes

These are the bands we hold across real geometries ‚Äî enclosures, brackets, housings ‚Äî not best-case test cubes:

| Feature                        | PETG / ASA        | PA12-CF           |
| ------------------------------ | ----------------- | ----------------- |
| General dimensions (per 100 mm) | ¬±0.3 mm          | ¬±0.25 mm          |
| Small features (< 20 mm)       | ¬±0.15 mm          | ¬±0.12 mm          |
| Vertical hole diameter         | ‚àí0.2 to ‚àí0.4 mm   | ‚àí0.2 to ‚àí0.3 mm   |
| Flatness (per 100 mm)          | 0.3‚Äì0.5 mm        | 0.2‚Äì0.4 mm        |
| Z height (per 100 mm)          | ¬±0.2 mm           | ¬±0.2 mm           |

Read the hole row carefully: printed holes are systematically **undersize**, not randomly off. Systematic errors are the good kind ‚Äî you compensate for them in CAD, as covered in [our FDM design guide](/en/blog/fdm-design-guide).

Direction matters as much as size. XY dimensions ride on belt accuracy and shrinkage; Z dimensions are quantized to the 0.2 mm layer height, so a 10.1 mm tall feature prints as either 10.0 or 10.2 ‚Äî never 10.1. If a height is critical, design it as a multiple of the layer height and you remove one error source entirely. And a caveat about the table itself: these are per-machine, per-material figures that assume a tuned shrinkage compensation profile. A generic slicer profile on an uncalibrated printer can easily double them, which explains most "FDM can't hold tolerance" stories.

## What drives the variance

Four effects dominate, and knowing them tells you which dimensions to trust.

**Thermal shrinkage.** Polymer leaves the nozzle above 230 ¬∞C and cools to room temperature; the part contracts around 0.3‚Äì0.6% for PETG and ASA. Carbon fiber cuts that roughly in half for PA12-CF ‚Äî around 0.1‚Äì0.3% ‚Äî which is why the filled nylon holds the tightest band in the table. Shrinkage scales with length: it's why tolerance is quoted per 100 mm.

**Corner and edge effects.** Contours pull inward at sharp external corners and swell slightly at internal ones. Large flat faces bow as upper layers shrink over lower ones ‚Äî that's the flatness row.

**First-layer squish.** The first layer is deliberately over-pressed into the bed for adhesion, flaring the bottom 0.2‚Äì0.5 mm of every wall outward by a tenth or two. Chamfer the bottom edge (0.5 mm is enough) and the flare disappears from your mating surfaces.

**The seam.** Each perimeter starts and stops somewhere, leaving a small ridge ‚Äî up to about 0.1‚Äì0.2 mm. On a cylindrical fit surface the seam is the local high point; if a shaft or pin must slide, plan the clearance for the seam, not the average diameter.

## Clearances for press, slide and loose fits

The table this article exists for. Diametral clearances between two printed parts (pin-in-hole; for printed-against-machined fits you can halve them):

| Fit          | Diametral clearance | Behavior                                | Typical use                        |
| ------------ | ------------------- | --------------------------------------- | ---------------------------------- |
| Press fit    | ‚àí0.1 to 0.0 mm      | Assembled with force, permanent         | Bearing seats, pins, inserts       |
| Snug fit     | +0.1 mm             | Hand pressure, holds position           | Locating bosses, lids that click   |
| Slide fit    | +0.2 to +0.3 mm     | Moves freely, minimal play              | Sliding trays, hinges, guides      |
| Loose fit    | +0.4 to +0.6 mm     | Always assembles, visible play          | Screw clearance, covers, hooks     |

Two special cases deserve their own numbers. Threads: don't print anything finer than M6 ‚Äî below that, tap a printed pilot hole or, better, use a heat-set brass insert (for M3 inserts, a √ò 4.0 mm hole in a boss at least 8 mm across). Snap fits: size the interference at 0.3‚Äì0.5 mm of deflection in PETG and keep the cantilever at least 8 mm long; ASA tolerates fewer cycles before the hook whitens at the layer seam.

Three rules make the table work in practice. Test the fit at the actual size ‚Äî a clearance that slides at √ò 5 mm binds at √ò 50 mm, because tolerance grew with the dimension while the clearance didn't. Put the adjustment in one part ‚Äî fix the hole, tune the pin; iterating both doubles your variables. And remember materials move: PETG and PA12 absorb moisture and grow up to a tenth of a percent, so a press fit sized in a dry week can crack a boss in a humid one. When a press fit matters, add a printed crush rib ‚Äî a 0.3 mm ridge that yields on assembly absorbs the whole tolerance band.

## When FDM is the wrong answer

FDM stops being the right process when a drawing carries machining expectations. ¬±0.05 mm across a part, flatness under 0.1 mm on a sealing face, bores that must be round to a few hundredths ‚Äî that's CNC territory, and pretending otherwise wastes two prototype rounds. We wrote up the full decision, with costs, in [printed PA12-CF vs machined aluminum](/en/compare/pa-cf-vs-aluminum).

The hybrid path is often the win: print the complex bracket in [PA12-CF](/en/materials/pa12-cf), then drill or ream the two bores that actually locate something. You get printed lead time and geometry freedom, and machined precision exactly where the function lives ‚Äî at a fraction of a fully machined part's price.
